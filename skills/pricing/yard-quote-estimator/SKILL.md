# Yard quote estimator

When a pooper scooper operator asks for help quoting a new customer, use this skill to help them think through everything that affects how long that yard will actually take to clean — and therefore what it should cost.

Every scooper business prices differently. Your job is not to spit out a number. Your job is to make sure the operator has thought through all the variables before they commit to a price. Walk them through it like a checklist, ask what you don't know, and help them land on a quote that's fair and protects their time.

---

## Step 1 — Figure out the actual yard size

Customers almost always describe their yard wrong. "Medium" to one person is a postage stamp to another. Never take their word for it without digging deeper.

**If the operator has the customer's address**, look it up on Google Maps and switch to satellite view. You can see the fenced yard area clearly from above and get a much more accurate read on the size than anything the customer tells you. Ask the operator to pull it up too if they can — walking through it together takes 30 seconds and saves a mispriced quote.

**If there's no address yet**, help the operator explain yard size to the customer in plain terms they'll actually understand. A good way to frame it:

- *Small* — roughly the size of a two-car garage or smaller. You can walk the whole thing in under a minute.
- *Medium* — roughly the size of a basketball court, give or take. A typical suburban backyard.
- *Large* — bigger than that. Multiple zones, a side yard, or just a lot of open space.

Even better: ask the customer how long it takes them to mow it. Under 10 minutes = small. 10–20 minutes = medium. 20+ minutes = large. That's a question customers can actually answer accurately.

---

## Step 2 — Ask about what's in the yard

The size of the yard is only part of the picture. What's *in* the yard often matters more. Ask the operator:

**Ground cover and obstacles:**
- Is it mostly grass, or are there gravel areas, mulch beds, or wood chips? Waste hides in gravel and mulch and takes significantly longer to clean — factor this in.
- Are there pine needles or heavy leaf cover? Waste disappears in leaves and needles. If it's fall or the yard has pine trees, this is a big deal. Some operators charge more seasonally or ask customers to rake before the visit.
- Is there a deck, patio furniture, a playset, or other stuff that creates tight spaces to work around?
- Any flower beds or landscaping the tech needs to be careful around?

**The dogs:**
- How many dogs and roughly what size? A Great Dane and a Chihuahua are not the same job.
- Are the dogs outside during the service or inside? Dogs underfoot slow everything down.
- Any aggression history or dogs that can't be around strangers? This is a safety question, not just a time question.

**Access:**
- Is there a gate? Does it latch properly or is it a two-handed wrestle every time?
- Does the tech need to go through the house or garage to reach the yard?
- Any combination locks or codes to note?

---

## Step 3 — Ask about the current state of the yard

This is the question most operators forget to ask — and it's the one that most often turns a 20-minute job into an hour.

Ask the operator to ask the customer: **when was the last time the yard was cleaned?**

If the answer is anything longer than two weeks, or if they're not sure, treat it as a first-clean situation. The first visit on a neglected yard is a completely different job than ongoing service. Common scenarios:

- **New customer, unknown history** — assume buildup, price the first visit higher or do a walkthrough before committing
- **Coming off another service provider** — ask why they're switching and when the last visit actually was
- **"A few weeks"** — dig in. A few weeks with two large dogs in a medium yard is a lot of waste.
- **Seasonal resumption** — a yard that's been dormant all winter is almost always a first-clean situation

It's worth having the operator explain this to the customer upfront: *"The first visit usually takes longer because we're starting from scratch — after that, regular visits go much faster."* Customers who understand this are far less likely to push back on a first-clean fee.

---

## Step 4 — Confirm the service details

Before building a quote, make sure you know:

- What frequency are they looking for — weekly, biweekly, monthly, or a one-time clean?
- Do they want waste removed from the property, or bagged and left at the curb?
- Any add-ons — deodorizer treatment, a gate check, anything else the operator offers?

Frequency changes everything. A yard that takes 15 minutes weekly will take 25–35 minutes biweekly and potentially 45–60 minutes monthly. Make sure the quote reflects the actual service being sold, not an idealized version of it.

---

## Step 5 — Help build the quote

Once the operator has answered these questions, help them think through the time estimate and work backwards to a price based on their own hourly target.

**Walk through it out loud with them.** For example:

> "Okay — medium yard, two labs, biweekly service, some gravel along the fence line, and it sounds like it hasn't been cleaned in about a month. I'd probably estimate the first visit at 45–50 minutes and ongoing visits at around 25–30 minutes once you're on a regular schedule. What do you need to make per hour to make that worth it?"

Let the operator name their number. Don't suggest a price for them — pricing varies too much by market, cost of living, and business model for a one-size-fits-all rate. Your job is to make sure the time estimate is accurate so whatever rate they charge actually works out.

**Remind them about minimums.** Most operators have a per-visit minimum regardless of how small the yard is, to cover drive time and overhead. If this stop is out of their way or a standalone visit, that minimum matters more.

---

## How to present the final quote to the customer

Help the operator explain the quote simply and confidently. A few things that make customers more likely to say yes:

- **Give a range, not a hard number, for the first visit.** "First clean will be $55–$70 depending on what we find, and then $38 per visit after that on a biweekly schedule." Ranges feel honest and set expectations.
- **Briefly explain why the first visit costs more.** Customers who understand it don't argue about it.
- **Be specific about what's included.** Waste removed from the property vs. bagged at the curb is a real difference to customers — spell it out.
- **Don't over-explain the pricing math.** One sentence is enough. If they ask, answer. If they don't, don't volunteer it.

---

## What not to do

- Don't let the customer define the yard size without at least cross-checking it on satellite view or asking the mowing question
- Don't skip the "how long since it was last cleaned" question — this is the one that bites operators most often
- Don't quote a flat biweekly price without flagging that the first visit will cost more
- Don't suggest a specific dollar rate — pricing is the operator's call based on their market and costs
- Don't forget to ask about leaves, pine needles, and gravel — these turn a quick yard into a slow one fast
